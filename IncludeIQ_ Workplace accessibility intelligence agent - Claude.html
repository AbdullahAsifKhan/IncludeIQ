What Problem Are We Actually Solving?

Imagine you are an HR manager at a mid-sized company. You genuinely want to be inclusive. You have a disability accommodation policy in your employee handbook. You have an ERG (Employee Resource Group) for employees with disabilities. You think you're doing fine.

But somewhere in your company right now, a job posting says "must be able to lift 30 lbs" for a desk job. A meeting transcript from last week has a manager casually saying "we need someone who can really hustle, no hand-holding." An onboarding document links to a PDF that is completely unreadable by a screen reader. A performance review template has no option to document accommodation requests.

None of that triggered an alert. No one flagged it. You don't even know it happened.

That is the problem. Companies have good intentions but zero systematic visibility into where their own processes, documents, and communications create barriers for people with disabilities. They only find out when someone files a complaint, quits quietly, or a lawsuit lands.

IncludeIQ changes that. It is an AI agent that reads across your workplace — job postings, HR documents, meeting notes, internal comms — and tells you exactly where the gaps are, why they are gaps, and what to do about them, with the legal and standards source cited.

The Goal in One Sentence

Give every HR team an always-on inclusion auditor that finds the gaps before they become lawsuits, attrition, or embarrassment — and tells them exactly how to fix each one.

Is This a Real Problem? The Proof.

The scale of who is affected is enormous

An estimated 1.3 billion people worldwide — about 16% of the global population — live with a disability. That is not a niche group. That is one in six people on the planet. Velocity Global

Inside the workforce, the gap is even sharper. In 2025, the unemployment rate for people with disabilities was 9.4%, compared to about 4.1% for people without disabilities. People with disabilities are more than twice as likely to be unemployed. That is not a coincidence — it reflects systemic barriers that start at the job posting and run through the entire employee lifecycle. High 5 Test

Companies are blind to how many of their own employees have disabilities

This is the most striking data point. A BCG survey of nearly 28,000 employees in 16 countries found that 25% of people said they have a disability or health condition that limits a major life activity. Meanwhile, most companies report that their workforce includes just 4% to 7% of employees with disabilities. Boston Consulting Group

Read that again. Companies think 4–7%. Reality is 25%. That is a 4–6x blind spot. Most of those people are simply not disclosing — often because they don't trust the company's ability to handle it without judgment.

Why don't people disclose? Because the environment doesn't feel safe.

Deloitte's first Disability Inclusion at Work 2024 report surveyed 10,000 respondents with disabilities across 20 countries and found concerns about negative perceptions when requesting workplace accommodations, requested accommodations being declined, work events that are not accessible, and experiences of non-inclusive behaviors — factors that combine to create an "access gap." Deloitte

That access gap is not caused by malice. It is caused by process failures — bad job postings, inaccessible documents, meetings that exclude, HR policies with gaps. Those are exactly the things IncludeIQ scans.

The legal and financial consequences are real and accelerating

Companies that miss these gaps do not just lose talent. They get sued. Here is a sample from EEOC actions in the last two years alone:

  • The Results Companies was required to pay $250,000 to settle an EEOC disability discrimination lawsuit after failing to accommodate an employee who needed screen reader technology to do her job. U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
  • Peak Performers was required to pay $160,000 after denying a brief unpaid leave request to an employee seeking treatment for a mental health disability, then firing her. U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
  • A jury awarded $1.675 million against McLane Northeast after the company refused to interview a Deaf candidate once it learned the candidate had a disability. U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission

In FY2024, the EEOC recorded 88,531 total discrimination charges, up 9.2% from FY2023. The trend is going up, not down. High 5 Test

Discrimination starts before the first interview — in the job posting

The consequences of discriminatory language in job postings can impede an organization financially and reputationally, including investigations, settlement agreements, and civil penalties. Third-party job boards may also be at risk and are responsible for the job postings placed on their sites. Orr Group

Most HR managers have no reliable way to check every posting before it goes live. They are doing this manually, inconsistently, or not at all.

Is Anyone Already Building This?

This is the important question. Here is the honest answer: not in the way IncludeIQ does it.

The existing tools fall into two separate buckets, and neither one does what this agent does:

Bucket 1 — Web accessibility scanners (WCAG tools) Tools like Axe, WAVE, and Workik scan websites and digital interfaces for screen reader issues, colour contrast failures, keyboard navigation problems. The best AI accessibility tools assist skilled practitioners with auditing, remediation guidance, and documentation work. What they cannot do is determine WCAG conformance on their own — conformance requires human evaluation. These tools look at websites, not HR processes, job descriptions, meeting transcripts, or internal comms. Completely different problem. Accessible

Bucket 2 — General compliance audit tools AI tools are starting to help internal audit teams flag financial control failures, ghost employees, and regulatory documentation gaps. A January 2025 flash poll of 2,574 internal auditors found that half believed controls testing and fieldwork would provide the greatest benefit from AI agents. These tools are built for financial and operational compliance — not disability inclusion specifically. AuditBoard

The gap IncludeIQ fills: There is currently no AI agent that does all of this together:

  • Reads job postings for exclusionary or discriminatory language
  • Scans meeting transcripts for non-inclusive patterns over time
  • Reviews HR documents and policies for accommodation gaps
  • Cross-references findings against ADA guidelines and WCAG standards with citations
  • Identifies systemic patterns across an organization rather than one-off issues
  • Produces a prioritized remediation roadmap

AI-powered tools can continuously monitor accessibility compliance in real-time — think of it as a proactive assistant that is always watching for potential risks and recommending fixes before regulators get involved. That vision exists in the digital accessibility world. No one has built it yet for the internal workplace inclusion world — for the documents, conversations, and processes where the real barriers live. Aigensei

Scenario 2 — Monthly meeting transcript scan

Microsoft uses Teams. Every meeting is automatically transcribed. IncludeIQ pulls the last 30 days of transcripts from a given team or org (with appropriate access controls) and looks for patterns.

It finds:

  • In six separate meetings, a specific manager's team consistently holds meetings at 7am or 8am. Employees with certain disabilities (chronic illness, medication schedules) are disproportionately disadvantaged by fixed early-morning scheduling. Flagged as a systemic pattern, not a one-off.
  • The phrase "just hop on a quick call" appears 47 times. No alternative async option is ever offered. Flagged as a potential barrier for employees with hearing impairments or communication processing differences.
  • One meeting has no transcript at all, meaning any deaf or hard-of-hearing employee had no access to what was discussed.

This is not about punishing anyone. It is about surfacing patterns that no one can see manually across thousands of meetings. The DEI team gets a monthly report. They can bring it to the relevant managers as coaching, not complaints.

Microsoft's Legal team needs to confirm their HR policies are current with ADA amendments and WCAG 2.2 standards before an annual review.

  • Identifies that the remote work accommodation policy was last updated in 2021 and does not reflect the ADA guidance updates from 2023
  • Finds that the mental health accommodation section uses clinical language that inadvertently creates a higher burden of proof than the law requires
  • Produces a prioritised list: fix these 3 things immediately (legal risk), these 5 things within 90 days (best practice gaps), these 8 things within a year (proactive improvements)

Legal gets a defensible audit trail. DEI gets an action plan. HR gets clear priorities.

Scenario 4 — A disabled job seeker uses the other side of the product

Instead of a disabled job seeker having to research all of this themselves, the agent does it for them in one place.

## The Problem

1.3 billion people worldwide live with a disability. A BCG survey of 28,000 employees found that 25% identify as having a disability — yet most companies report only 4–7% of their workforce does. That gap is not a counting error. It is a trust problem. Talented people are not disclosing, not asking for accommodations, and quietly leaving — because the workplace environment signals it is not safe to do so.

The root cause is not bad intentions. It is invisible process failures. A job posting with a line that quietly excludes wheelchair users. A meeting schedule that never offers an async option for employees with chronic illness. An HR policy that was last updated before the 2023 ADA guidance changes. No one flagged any of it. No one even saw it.

HR teams are overwhelmed and cannot audit every document, every transcript, every policy manually. There is currently no systematic tool that does this for them.

## What IncludeIQ Does

IncludeIQ is a multi-step reasoning agent that reads workplace documents and finds disability inclusion gaps before they cause harm.

**Input**: Job postings, meeting transcripts, HR policy documents

**Reasoning chain**:
1. Work IQ reads the document and identifies language patterns that may exclude or disadvantage people with disabilities
2. Foundry IQ retrieves the exact ADA guideline, WCAG standard, or EEOC rule that applies — every finding is grounded with a real citation, no hallucination
3. Fabric IQ cross-references findings across all documents to identify systemic patterns, not just one-off mistakes

**Output**: A prioritised, sourced remediation roadmap — what to fix, why it matters legally, and exactly how to fix it

## Why It Wins on the Judging Criteria

- **Accuracy and relevance** — Solves a documented, measurable problem backed by Deloitte, BCG, and EEOC data
- **Multi-step reasoning** — The full plan → retrieve → reason → cite chain is visible and traceable
- **Reliability and safety** — Foundry IQ citation grounding eliminates hallucination on legal content
- **Creativity** — No existing tool audits internal workplace communications and HR documents for disability inclusion gaps at the process level
- **UX** — Live reasoning trace makes the agent's thinking visible; clean card output makes findings actionable in seconds

## The Two Sides of the Product

**Employer side**: Companies upload their documents and get a continuous, cited audit of their workplace inclusion gaps across hiring, communications, and policy

**Job seeker side**: Disabled job seekers input their accommodation needs and the agent scans available postings to identify which companies are genuinely accessible versus which ones have red flags — before they apply

## Real Stakes

- 88,531 EEOC discrimination charges filed in FY2024, up 9.2% year on year
- ADA lawsuits rose 27% in 2025 to 3,117 federal filings
- Amazon faced a 33-page letter from 200+ disabled employees in 2025 alleging systemic accommodation failures driven by automated systems
- Google was sued by its own Deaf employee after being used as a diversity showcase while her accommodation requests were repeatedly denied
- Microsoft paid $14.4 million in 2024 to settle a disability and parental leave discrimination case in California

These companies did not set out to exclude anyone. They just had no system to catch what they were missing. IncludeIQ is that system.

- Microsoft Work IQ — reads emails, meeting transcripts, and communications for inclusion pattern analysis
- Microsoft Foundry IQ — grounds every finding in cited ADA, WCAG, and EEOC sources with zero hallucination
- Microsoft Fabric IQ — identifies systemic patterns across an organisation's document corpus, not just individual one-off issues
- React frontend with live reasoning trace visualisation
- Fully accessible UI — the product about accessibility is itself accessible

How The Whole Thing Is Connected

User uploads a document on the React frontend. React sends that document as text to your Python backend via a simple API call. Step One — Build The Backend First

Create a file called main.py. This is your entire backend to start with.

Write one endpoint called /analyse. It accepts a POST request with the document text inside. It sends that text to Azure AI Foundry with a system prompt. It returns the findings as JSON.

Your system prompt is the most important thing you will write in this whole project. It should say something like this:

"You are an expert workplace accessibility auditor. You know ADA Title I and Title II, WCAG 2.2 standards, and EEOC guidelines in detail. When given a workplace document, identify every gap that could exclude or disadvantage people with disabilities. For each gap you find, return the exact line from the document that is problematic, the specific law or standard it violates with the section number, a plain English explanation of why it is a problem, and a suggested rewrite. Return your response as structured JSON only."

That prompt is doing the work of your Foundry IQ layer. It is grounding the output in real law. It is what stops the agent from hallucinating.

Test this endpoint with Postman or curl before you touch the frontend at all. Send it a job posting with three deliberate problems in it. Make sure the JSON comes back clean. Fix the prompt until it does. This is your entire day one.

Step Two — Add The Three IQ Layers As Separate Functions

Once the basic endpoint works, split the logic into three separate Python functions. This is how you demonstrate all three IQ layers to the judges.

Function one is called work_iq_scan. It takes the document text and identifies the suspicious patterns. Language that might exclude people. Missing accommodation statements. Rigid physical requirements that are not essential to the job. This is your Work IQ simulation.

Function two is called foundry_iq_retrieve. It takes each pattern found by function one and retrieves the specific legal citation that applies. You can hardcode a reference library of the most common ADA, WCAG, and EEOC rules as a Python dictionary. When the agent finds a problem, it looks up the matching law. This is your Foundry IQ grounding layer.

Function three is called fabric_iq_analyse. It takes all findings across all documents and checks for repetition. If the same pattern appears in more than one document, it flags it as systemic rather than accidental. This is your Fabric IQ pattern layer.

Each function runs in sequence. Work IQ runs first. Foundry IQ runs second on the output of Work IQ. Fabric IQ runs third on the combined output of everything. That sequential chain is your visible reasoning trace. This is exactly what the judges are looking for.

Step Three — Build The Frontend

Build your three screens in order.

Screen one is the upload page. A logo at the top. Three text areas below it, one for each document type. Labels that say Job Posting, Meeting Transcript, and HR Policy. One button that says Analyse. When the button is clicked, it sends the text to your backend and navigates to screen two.

Step three, retrieving legal citations. Step four, checking for systemic patterns. Each string appears with a small animated tick when it completes. While this plays out, your actual API call is running in the background. When the API returns, you navigate to screen three.

Screen three is the results. Map over your JSON array of findings. Each finding becomes a card. Each card shows the problematic line, the law that applies, the severity tag in colour, and the suggested fix. Three summary numbers at the top. A download button at the bottom.

That is your entire frontend. Build it in that order. Do not start screen three before screen two works.
